The 37 Bus Is Late Again, And I Miss You

Nightfall
in February. I can’t
seem to make up
my mind. I don
and shed my
gloves; the wind
gives me flack
for it. I stare
into the street’s
yawning mouth
and wait for things
to come my way. Go
my way. Two more
minutes in the cold
and then I’ll start
walking, I say, and many
two-more-minutes idly
pass. I’m a fretful
liar. Misfortune indulges
my appetite for righteous
complaint. Old pains
overwhelm me with
sentimentality. I wonder,
sometimes, what frightens me
away from novelty,
from beautiful strangers
with whom I have developed
no rapport, against whom
I have devised no
stratagems. I see the past
rounding the corner,
that shining, thrilling
wound, terribly
familiar. It’s easy
to say I want
a good life. Easier,
still, to go
home.
